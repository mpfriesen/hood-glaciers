Mt. Hood’s Glaciers

A Mountain’s
Disappearing Ice

Mark Friesen
Geography 778, Spring 2022
University of Wisconsin - Madison

Like ice everywhere in our warming world, the glaciers of Oregon’s Mt. Hood are melting away. By most estimates, the glacial mass on the mountain is at least half of what it was at the beginning of the 20th century. And it may be gone entirely by the end of this one.

Never having seen a map that quantified this loss, I went looking, only to find that there essentially isn’t one. Current maps, even those produced by the USGS, still use glacier extents that were originally drawn in the 1950s.

Scholarly work, particularly by researchers at Portland State University, will soon correct that. In the meantime, this is my humble attempt.

The USGS topo map of Mt. Hood from 1962 had glacier extents drawn in 1956, showing more than 22 square kilometers covered by glaciers.

In the 2020 map, 58 years later, the extents are unchanged.

In reality, glaciers today cover fewer than 8 square kilometers of the peak, a loss of 66 percent.

In 1956, the Zigzag Glacier was the largest on the mountain at more than 3 square kilometers.

It’s now at 395,000 square meters, a loss of more than 87 percent.

Sixty-five years ago, the Sandy Glacier was more than 1.4 million square meters.

It’s now shrunk nearly 60 percent to 592,000 square meters.

The famous Palmer Glacier near Timberline Lodge was once more than a million square meters.

At 98,000 square meters today, 91 percent smaller, it is little more than a snowfield and no longer meets the scientific definition of a glacier.
